## Sources

### Ordnance Survey

[Ordnance Survey](https://www.ordnancesurvey.co.uk/about) (OS) is Great
Britain’s National Mapping Service. OS Open Data provides free
access to a variety of geographic datasets for Great Britain under the Open
Government Licence (OGL).

#### OS VectorMap District

[OS VectorMap District](https://docs.os.uk/os-downloads/contextual-or-derived-mapping/os-vectormap-district)
OS VectorMap District is an open simple vector dataset with a nominal scale of
1:25&nbsp;000 covering the whole of Great Britain.

- **Surface water**  
  Small lakes and small islands in waterbodies are not included.
- **Woodlands**  
  Small areas of woodland are omitted and small clearings in woodland are filled
- **Ornaments**  
  Simplified versions of artwork originally drawn on paper maps to depict coastal rocks, outcropping rocks, boulders, cartographic slopes, and scree
- **Spot heights**  
  Elevation measurement, in most cases at the summit of a hill or mountain
- **Named places**  
  Settlement or geographic place names

#### OS Terrain 50

[OS Terrain 50](https://docs.os.uk/os-downloads/height-and-imagery/os-terrain-50)
is an open height dataset of contours with spot heights, breaklines, coastline,
lakes, ridges, and formlines for Great Britain.

- **Contour lines**
- **Hillshading** (generated from grid data)
- **Color relief** (generated from grid data)

The contour lines included in OS Terrain 50 are comparable to the contours
on an OS Landranger map. (That is more precise than I would expect contour
lines generated from a 50-meter grid to be.)

### OpenStreetMap

[OpenStreetMap](https://www.openstreetmap.org/about) (OSM) is a free, editable
map of the whole world that is being built by volunteers largely from scratch
and released with an open-content license.

- **Ocean**
- **Landcover**
- **Landuse**
- **Parks**
- **Administrative boundaries**
- **Airports**
- **Roads**
- **Railways**
- **Aerial ways**  
  Various forms of transport for passengers and goods that use wires, including
  cable cars, gondolas, chair lifts, drag lifts, and zip lines.
- **Shipping lines**
- **Buildings**
- **Place names**  
  Sometimes replaced with OS data
- **Points of interest**  
  Mostly amenities, sports, shops, and tourist locations.

### The Database of British and Irish Hills

The [Database of British and Irish Hills](https://www.hills-database.co.uk/database_notes.html)
(DoBIH) has the intention of providing the most accurate hill data available.
Their data are accepted by Ordnance Survey. Well-publicised survey results
include the reclassifications of Sgurr nan Ceannaichean and Beinn a' Chlaidheimh
from Munro to Corbett.

- **Hill**  
  For some hills this map uses Ordnance Survey names instead of DoBIH names, but
  all other hill data always comes from DoBIH.
  DoBIH removes accents in Gaelic and Welsh names to facilitate searching.

### Natural Earth

[Natural Earth](https://www.naturalearthdata.com/) is a worldwide public domain
map dataset built through a collaboration of many volunteers and supported by
NACIS (North American Cartographic Information Society).

This map uses Natural Earth for simplified features to display at low zooms.

- **Ocean**
- **Lakes**
- **Waterways**
- **Administrative Boundaries**
- **Place names**
- **Antarctic ice shelf**
- **Glaciated areas**
- **Urban areas**

## Inspiration

The general appearance of this map is inspired by the Explorer, Landranger, and
British Mountain Maps I've used.

[John Thorne's description](https://www.walkhighlands.co.uk/news/walkhighlands-map-server-and-tiles/)
of the open data he used to make the Walkhighlands map tiles helped me realize
sufficient open data sources exist to make a usable walking map of the Scottish
hills.
